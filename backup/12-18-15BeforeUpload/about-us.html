About

Since 2003, students at Carlmont High School in Belmont, CA have joined Woodside High School’s FRC Team 100 because we did not have a shop of our own. However, in 2015, Woodside's administration decidd to focus on having only Woodside students on Team 100, reverting back to a team like it was when it was started in 1995.
So Deep Blue Robotics was created to support the Carlmont High School Students.

The 34 Carlmont students currently on Team 199 are highly motivated, and are working hard to create an FRC team from scratch. The students of Carlmont High School currently face the challenge of creating a shop with limited funding, as Carlmont’s administration has indicated that it cannot supply the team with any funds. Therefore, we are actively searching for corporate sponsors and personal donors. As well as securing funding, we are also searching for team mentors to teach and guide the students of the team as they tackle the challenges inherent in building a complex robot in just six weeks.

Our mission statement: The mission of the Carlmont Robotics FRC team is to provide an opportunity for students to learn engineering, project management, and community relations skills in an environment that is fun, yet competitive and stressful at times in order to simulate real world situations and prepare our students for real-world projects.

FRC

Every year, over 3,000 robotics teams from high schools all across the world each build a unique robot to compete in an annual competition, organized by FIRST ("For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology"). This competition is called FRC ("FIRST Robotics Competition") and begins early in January, when FIRST releases the game challenge that teams’ robots must perform. Following the release of the game, teams have just six weeks to design, fabricate, program, and test the robot to be ready to compete.

FIRST is a large non-profit organization that holds robotics competitions for high school students throughout the United States and around the world. FIRST stands for “For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology” because its challenging competitions encourage students to innovate and develop their technical skills in a competitive, fun, and rewarding environment.

Over 3,000 teams participated in the FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC) this year, and that number is quickly growing because of the large number of parents, teachers, and students who realize what an outstanding learning opportunity this program provides. Each year a game is announced and teams are given roughly six weeks to design and build a robot that can compete effectively in that game. After the 6 weeks are over, the robot, whether it is finished or not, is put into a large bag that cannot be opened until competition time.

In the process of making the robot, students learn essential STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math) skills, such as programming, electronics, CAD (Computer Aided Design), mechanics, and more. Students learn these skills from other experienced students and from adult mentors, many of whom are professionals in their fields. Students also learn critical teamwork and working skills, such as communicating effectively, meeting deadlines, and leadership skills. Participating in the robotics program nurtures and prepares students for both college and careers in modern technology.

As well as as FRC teams, other FIRST teams include FTC (FIRST Tech Challenge) teams for 7-12 graders, and FLL (FIRST Lego League) teams for grades four to eight.

Team Management

Information about how the team is organized.

 

 

Our six week “build season” is a very stressful and often chaotic time, and we need to stay organized and focused in order to produce a quality robot. Drawing from lessons learned in our experience on Team 100, our captain and leadership team designed a new management structure, with the goal of being able to fully utilize our resources and work efficiently during build season. Ideally, under this system, everyone can be engaged and the robot can be built to our specifications as quickly as possible.

In creating our team’s management structure, we considered the following questions:

  • What is the most important part of the build season?
  • How can we make decisions quickly and efficiently, but still keep a democracy and brainstorm effectively, allowing everyone a chance to share their ideas?
  • How can we create a system that balances the skills of the experienced and more knowledgeable students and those of the less experienced, newer students?
  • How can we create a system where students are aware of what each other are doing, so we can work on tasks simultaneously and always know the next step in building the robot?
  • How can we ensure that the students on the team are learning as much as they can?
  • What should the level of mentor involvement on the team be?
Our answers to these can be divided into three sections:
  1. Team leadership structure and dynamic
  2. Active project management
  3. Mentor involvement
